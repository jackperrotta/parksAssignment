History of Public Conservation

Sadly our efforts as a nation are getting worse :(

The U.S. has a total of 59 protected national parks, including Yellowstone National Park, labeled as the first national park in 1872. Since then, other beloved grounds, such as Yosemite, Mesa Verde, the Everglades, and the Grand Canyon, have been protected by the U.S. Congress and the National Parks and Wildlife Services to ensure the protection of animals, ecosystems, and geographic cultures alike. 27 U.S. states have national parks, with California having the most with a total of 9. National parks have not only become some of the most beautiful tourist locations, but have become home to many research and conservation centers. The expansive set of national parks have also opened up doors for education on fire management, wildlife biodiversity, and the preservation of natural resources.

In 2009, Barack Obama was inaugurated as the 44th U.S. president, and ran his administration as a conservationist of wildlife and national parks. 548 million acres of land were placed under protection throughout the 8 years of Obamaâ€™s presidency. Obama also set the record for establishing the most protected habitats in presidential history, even topping Theodore Roosevelt, the founder of U.S. National Parks. Obama labeled 22 additional national parks in his presidency, and expanded millions of acres in land reserves for existing parks despite backlash from the oil and gas industries. Obama also implemented impressive internal initiatives within national parks such as prohibiting the selling of plastic bottles in national parks and pushing forward laws to fight against illegal fishing of sharks and other marine life.

Our Mission

Protect Our Public Lands

With the Trump Administration and head of the Department of the Interior, Rep. Ryan Zinke, the threat to maintain our public lands, especially National Parks, is at an all time high.

As U.S. citizens, Empower Parks believes that we all have a social responsibility to ensure our public land receive proper funding for the protection of animals, natural resources, park employees, and local economic stability made possible by the U.S. National Park Service.

If we do not take action now:

  • Resources will be exhausted for captain gain
  • Wildlife will be displaced or killed
  • National tourism industry will decline
